ElectionData.MY began the same way most projects of this nature do: not as a grand plan, but rather as a series of events that eventually snowballed into something bigger. In 2022, I casually started [tweeting data about the upcoming election](https://x.com/Thevesh/status/1585248940425105409), and for some reason it struck a chord. Suddenly, people were calling me the 'election data guy', and I felt that I had to put in the time and effort to live up to these expectations.

After the election, I decided to sit down and try to compile an election data collection which I could use a source of truth (at this point, still just for the purpose of tweeting). That was when I ran into a wall - a clean source for Malaysian election results simply did not exist. They were scattered across PDFs, inconsistently formatted Excel/CSV files (heroically compiled by civil society, I might add), and for some older elections, physical reports/gazettes stored in SPR's archives. In short, no single place held it all, and what did exist was generally nowhere near the modern definition of good open data.

That was just the election results, at Parliament and DUN level. For everything else (constituency maps, voter rolls, voter demographics, saluran-level data, voting-district maps, etc), there was essentially nothing in the public domain, partly because SPR systemically limits the availability of such information, and partly because what we were talking about was really a digital reconstruction of 70 years of electoral history.

## And so, the long nights began.

Once I realised the scale of the work I had taken on, I decided - essentially immediately - that I was going to do it properly, and open-source everything. And so, what started as a personal collection became the Malaysian Election Corpus, or MECo. ElectionData.MY is its public face: a platform for accessing, querying, and building on comprehensive data about every election held in Malaysia from 1954 to the present.

The scope covers every candidate and party that has ever contested a Malaysian election. Parlimen and DUN-level results, geospatial boundaries, pseudonymised voter rolls, saluran-level breakdowns. All of it free, all of it in the public domain.

<ArticleBody>

ElectionData.MY was born as one person's long-running bet that making data available changes what people can do with it. But it grew into a product that stands on the shoulders of dozens of people who supported its growth and development. Our collective hope is that researchers ask better questions, developers build better tools, and above all else, that voters have better data with which to make decisions and understand Malaysia's democracy.

The longer-term hope is simpler still: that SPR eventually does what we are doing, and this platform therefore becomes unnecessary.

Until then, the collection continues. Here, I make an open offer to SPR - if SPR wishes to adopt this platform as its own, I stand ready to curate the datasets, build and maintain the site, and do whatever is necessary to ensure proper institutional embedding within SPR, including training SPR's data/tech teams. This offer stands unconditionally and without expiry, completely pro bono.

My goal was never to get credit for this. It was to make sure it exists.
